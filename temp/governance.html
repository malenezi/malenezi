Governance & Standards

The standing machinery that keeps the framework accurate, current, and credible — governance bodies, refresh cycles, and quality assurance.

Why governance matters

A living framework needs a living discipline.

In a field that moves as fast as AI, the central risk is drift — a curriculum that quietly falls out of date and credentials that lose their meaning. The framework is built modularly so it can be updated cheaply. But modularity only pays off if a governance discipline actually triggers the updates.

That discipline is set out here: defined governance bodies, a tiered refresh calendar, and quality assurance measured against outcomes.

Governance bodies

Four bodies, clear ownership.

The framework is governed by four standing bodies, each with a defined remit and review cadence.

Quarterly

Curriculum Steering Committee

Owns the ecosystem. Approves new pathways, major changes, and the annual plan. Includes Academy leadership and employer representation.

Per refresh cycle

Competency Review Panels

One per specialty area. Subject-matter experts who re-validate that competencies and modules still match the occupation and the labour market.

Quarterly

Assessment & Quality Board

Owns assessment standards, rubric calibration, assessor certification, and the integrity of every credential issued.

Twice yearly

Industry Advisory Council

Employers and government entities. Supplies labour-market signal and validates the framework's demand indicators.

The refresh cycle

Reviewed in tiers, never all at once.

Each competency tier is refreshed at a cadence matched to how fast its knowledge ages — so review effort is steady and nothing is ever badly stale.

Tier 1 · Technical

Every 6 months

Tools, frameworks, and applied practice — the fastest-moving layer. Concepts hold; the applied content is refreshed.

Fastest cadence
Tier 2 · Responsible AI

Every 12 months

Ethics, governance, and compliance — refreshed annually, and immediately whenever Saudi AI or data-protection regulation changes.

Or on regulatory change
Tier 3 · Enablers

Every 24 months

Communication, collaboration, and strategic thinking — the most durable competencies, reviewed least often.

Slowest cadence
Quality assurance

Measured through three lenses.

The framework's health is measured by outcomes — not enrolment counts. Every metric connects to a decision.

  • Completion rate — the share of learners who finish a started module or stage.
  • Skills gain — proficiency measured before and after, against the competency matrix.
  • Time-to-competency — actual time to a credential against the pathway estimate.
  • Employment outcome — learners in a relevant role within six months of credentialing.
  • Employer satisfaction — employer rating of credential-holders' on-the-job capability.
  • Time-to-productivity — how fast a credentialed hire reaches full productivity.
  • Retention — retention of credentialed staff at 12 and 24 months.
  • Credential trust — the share of employers recognising the credential in hiring.
  • Modularity index — the average number of pathways each module serves.
  • Update agility — time from a review decision to the updated module going live.
  • Cost-per-competency — total ecosystem cost divided by competencies evidenced.
  • NOSF coverage — the share of occupation tasks covered by a live, validated module.
