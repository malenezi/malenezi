About the Framework

Why a national Data & AI competency framework exists, how it was built, and the principles that hold it together.

The purpose

One standard for a national capability.

Saudi Arabia's Vision 2030 depends on a workforce fluent in data and artificial intelligence — across government, the private sector, and society. Capability at that scale cannot be built course by course. It needs a single, coherent reference.

The Data & AI Competency Framework is that reference. It maps occupational competencies to stackable, credential-bearing learning pathways, so that every learner — from a citizen taking their first AI course to a Chief AI Officer — moves through one connected system with a shared definition of what capability means.

What the framework delivers
  • A common language for Data & AI roles and skills nationwide
  • Clear, navigable career progression for every learner
  • Verifiable credentials that employers and universities trust
  • A modular curriculum that updates without full rewrites
  • Workforce-planning intelligence for government entities
How it was built

A rigorous, three-stage methodology.

The framework follows the same comprehensive methodology used to develop the National Occupational Standard Framework — expert-led, evidence-based, and validated.

Stage 01 · Define

Occupations & competencies defined with SMEs

Subject-matter experts define each occupation's tasks, skills, knowledge, and abilities, drawing on job-market analysis and the experience of leading international frameworks.

Source: SDAIA national experts & sector SMEs
Stage 02 · Map

Competencies mapped to pathways & modules

Each competency is mapped to proficiency levels, observable behaviours, assessment methods, and the modular programs that develop it — composing the stackable learning architecture.

Output: pathways, modules, credentials
Stage 03 · Validate

Three-stage expert validation

Validated first by SDAIA SMEs, then by national SMEs across the digital, financial, health, and education sectors — with feedback reflected before release.

Status: pilot for testing & feedback
Design principles

Five commitments, in every layer.

These principles are not aspirations bolted on at the end — they are designed into the structure of the framework itself.

Principle 01

Stackable progression

Every credential is valuable alone and counts toward a larger one — no learner ever accumulates study that leads nowhere.

Principle 02

Multiple entry & exit

A diagnostic routes each learner to the right starting point; named exit points mean a learner can stop at any employable level.

Principle 03

Responsible AI, gated

Ethics, bias mitigation, and data protection are a hard gate before specialisation — never an optional later add-on.

Principle 04

Modular & maintainable

Competency-mapped modules can be updated on a schedule without rewriting whole programs or triggering re-accreditation.

Principle 05

Built for Saudi Arabia

Vision 2030 alignment, Saudi-context resources, Arabic/English delivery, and PDPL compliance are designed in from the start.

Open standards

No vendor lock-in

Open tooling and the Open Badges 3.0 credential standard keep the ecosystem portable and interoperable across platforms and borders.

Benchmarked globally

Local standard, international calibration.

The framework was developed by reviewing leading international practice — so a credential earned in Riyadh is calibrated against the frameworks employers know worldwide.

This international calibration is what makes the framework's credentials portable, and what supports Vision 2030's goal of an internationally competitive workforce.
